Lecture 1 · Foundation
What is Reinforcement Learning?
Learning by doing — no textbook, no labels, just interaction and feedback.

The Bicycle Analogy

Dr. Sanghita opened the course with a deceptively simple question: how did you learn to ride a bicycle? Nobody handed you a textbook. Nobody told you the exact angle to hold your handlebars or the precise force to apply. You simply got on the bike, fell, adjusted, and tried again. That entire process — learning by doing, guided entirely by feedback from the world around you — is the essence of reinforcement learning.

🚲 Key Insight

Every time you fell off the bicycle, that fall was a negative reward telling you "this approach doesn't work." Every time you balanced for a second longer, that was a positive reward. You were not given a rule book — you discovered the rules through interaction with the environment.

What RL Is — and Is Not

A common misconception is that reinforcement learning is a type of neural network or classifier. Dr. Sanghita was emphatic here: RL is a learning paradigm, not a model type. The mathematical function doing the learning inside could be a neural network, a decision tree, or a simple lookup table — but the strategy of learning from environmental interaction is what defines RL.

✅
RL IS
A learning technique. A strategy. A paradigm for sequential decision-making through environmental interaction.
❌
RL IS NOT
A neural network. A classifier. A clustering method. A decision tree. It can use these — but it is not one of them.
🎯
When to Use RL
When no dataset exists, when the problem is sequential, and when you have a goal that can be represented as a reward function.
🍳 The Cooking Analogy (used throughout the course)

You cook upma for your family with no cookbook. Their reaction — "too salty," "not enough oil," "this is perfect!" — is your reward signal. Each time you cook, you adjust based on that feedback. After several iterations you arrive at the perfect recipe. That final recipe is your policy.

Next up: the 7 core elements that every RL problem is built from.

Lecture 1 · Core Vocabulary
The 7 Elements of RL
Every RL problem ever built uses exactly these seven building blocks.

Before writing a single line of code or deriving any algorithm, you need to be fluent in the seven fundamental components of any RL system. These are the vocabulary of the field, and every algorithm you will encounter — from Q-Learning to PPO — is just a different strategy for working with these same seven things.

🤖
Agent
The learner and decision-maker. It perceives the environment, chooses actions, and receives rewards. In the vacuum example: the robot. In chess: the player.
🌍
Environment
Everything outside the agent. It responds to actions by changing state and delivering rewards. In the vacuum example: the two rooms A and B.
📸
State (S)
The current situation — "the picture the robot takes of the world around it." In the vacuum: (current room, clean/dirty). State is what the agent observes.
⚡
Action (A)
The choices available to the agent. In the vacuum: Move Left, Move Right, Suck. Actions are what the agent does in each state.
🏆
Reward (R)
Scalar feedback from the environment. Can be positive, negative, immediate, delayed, sparse, or noisy. The hero of RL — the only learning signal.
🗺️
Policy (π)
The strategy. π(a|s) = probability of taking action a in state s. The ultimate output of every RL algorithm — the agent's "rulebook" for behaviour.
📈
Value Function
V(s) or Q(s,a) — estimates expected future reward. Guides long-term decision-making when immediate rewards are sparse or equal. Covered in depth in Lecture 2.

The Two Key Probabilities

When the environment is stochastic (uncertain), two probability distributions govern what happens. Understanding the difference is essential for every algorithm you will study.

Policy (Agent's behaviour)
π(a | s) = P(Action = a | State = s)
Model Dynamics (Environment's response)
P(s', r | s, a) = P(next state = s', reward = r | state = s, action = a)
Deterministic vs Stochastic

Deterministic: one action always leads to the same next state. Chess is mostly deterministic — moving a knight always lands on the same square.

Stochastic: one action can lead to different next states with different probabilities. Stocks are stochastic — investing ₹1000 today might yield profit or loss depending on market forces outside your control.

Types of Rewards

TypeDescriptionExample
ImmediateReceived right after the actionWinning a chess coin after a move
DelayedComes only after several stepsExam result after 8 weeks of studying
SparseRare or infrequentReward only when the full task is complete
Non-stationaryChanges with time / environmentStock market returns — vary day to day

Now let's look at Policy in more depth.

Lecture 1 · Core Concept
Policy & the Goal of RL
The policy is the final recipe — the strategy the agent develops after all learning is done.

If you understand only one concept from this entire course, it should be policy. Everything in RL — every algorithm, every formula, every experiment — exists for one purpose: to find the optimal policy. The policy answers the fundamental question: "Given where I am right now, what should I do?"

Policy Definition
π(a | s) = Probability of taking action a when in state s

Deterministic vs Stochastic Policy

A deterministic policy says: "In state S, always take action A." Think of a chess endgame where one move is obviously optimal — there is no uncertainty. A stochastic policy says: "In state S, take action A1 with 95% probability and A2 with 5%." This is used when the environment is uncertain and the agent must hedge its bets slightly.

The Convergence Goal

RL algorithms start with a random, highly uncertain stochastic policy (50-50 on everything) and progressively sharpen it. The goal of convergence is to arrive at a policy that is almost deterministic — e.g., "take action A with 99% confidence." That 1% residual keeps the agent adaptable if the world changes unexpectedly.

The Objective of RL — Formally

The entire mission of an RL agent is to find the policy that maximises expected cumulative reward over time. In the chess analogy, you are not just trying to win the next move — you are trying to accumulate as many opponent coins as possible throughout the game so that you eventually achieve checkmate.

The RL Objective
π* = argmaxπ E[R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ... + Rₙ]

How Policy and Value Functions Relate

Policy and value functions are what Dr. Sanghita called a "close-knit family." You compute value functions to evaluate how good your current policy is. Then you use those values to improve the policy. The improved policy generates new experience. New experience updates the value functions. This cycle repeats until both converge — that is what RL training actually looks like in practice.

Lecture 2 · Key Concept
Value Functions
Why immediate rewards aren't enough — and how value functions look into the future.

The Problem with Rewards Alone

You might think: if the goal of RL is to maximise reward, then just always pick the action with the highest immediate reward, right? The chess example Dr. Sanghita introduced demolishes this intuition completely.

Imagine you are mid-game at state S1. You have two moves: Move A leading to S2, and Move B leading to S3. Recall that in chess, the reward design is: win = +1, loss = −1, every other mid-game move = 0. Both your moves are mid-game moves, so both give you a reward of zero. The immediate reward is completely useless here — it cannot distinguish between a path that leads to checkmate in 5 moves and one that leads to losing your queen in 3 moves.

Three moves from here, the agent loses its queen — a costly mistake. The value 3 already accounts for this future loss. Avoid Move B, despite the same immediate reward of 0.
🔑 The Core Distinction

Reward R = immediate feedback for the last action. Myopic.
Value Function V(s) / Q(s,a) = expected total future reward from this point forward. Forward-looking. Value functions capture future reward directions — when the value of S3 is computed as 3, the future queen loss is already baked into that number.

V(s) vs Q(s,a) — Which One to Use?

FunctionDefinitionUse When
V(s)Expected future reward starting from state s, following current policyYou can model the environment — evaluate states first, then derive policy from state values
Q(s,a)Expected future reward for taking action a in state s, then following policyYou cannot model the environment (most real-world cases: autonomous driving, robotics). Q directly tells you what to do.
Why Q-Learning uses Q, not V

In autonomous driving, you cannot write down a complete model of the environment — there are too many variables (other drivers, weather, road conditions). So you cannot compute V(s) easily because you don't know the transition probabilities. Q(s,a), however, can be estimated directly from experience: "every time I was in state S and took action A, here's what reward I got on average." Q-learning, the most famous RL algorithm, is built entirely around estimating Q(s,a) from experience.

Next: the K-Armed Bandit — a simplified RL problem that isolates the action-selection challenge.

Lecture 2 · Framework
The K-Armed Bandit
Stripping RL down to its pure action-selection essence — no states, just choices.

To understand how an agent decides which action to take, Dr. Sanghita temporarily removed all the complexity of state transitions from the picture. Forget about the environment changing — imagine you are always in the same state and simply have K different actions to choose from. Which one do you pick to maximise reward? This is the K-Armed Bandit Problem.

🎰 Why "Bandit"?

The name comes from casino slot machines, colloquially called "one-armed bandits" (one lever = one arm, bandit = they take your money). A row of K such machines is a K-armed bandit. Each machine has its own unknown payout distribution. The player must decide which levers to pull to maximise total winnings — with limited plays. Each lever is one action; each pull is one time step.

The Action Value Estimate

Since the true reward distribution of each action is unknown, the agent must estimate it. The simplest and most principled way is to take the running average of all rewards received from that action so far.

Action Value Estimate Q(a)
Q(a) = Σ(rewards when action a was taken) ÷ (number of times a was taken)

This is just a sample mean. By the law of large numbers, with enough trials, Q(a) converges to the true action value q*(a) — but only under one critical assumption.

⚠️ The Stationarity Assumption

The running average is a good estimate only if the reward distribution is stationary — meaning the expected reward for each action does not change over time. A casino with fixed payouts is stationary. The real stock market, a patient's response to medicine, or restaurant food quality are all non-stationary. For non-stationary settings, a weighted average (discounting old rewards) is needed — a topic for a future session.

The Arjun / Food Stalls Analogy

Dr. Sanghita made this concrete with a relatable example: Arjun is a new IT employee with 10 food stalls in his office cafeteria. He has never tried any of them. His goal — high satisfaction from lunch — is the reward. The 10 stalls are his 10 actions. He has no data to start with, so he explores all 10 first. After that, he can use his accumulated average ratings to make smarter choices. This is exactly how a K-Armed Bandit agent works.

Now comes the hardest question: once you have estimates, should you always pick the best one?

Lecture 2 · Core Dilemma
Exploration vs. Exploitation
The fundamental tension at the heart of every RL algorithm ever written.

Once an agent has some estimates of action values, it faces the most fundamental dilemma in reinforcement learning: should it exploit its current best knowledge (pick the highest estimated action) or explore (try a different action to gather more information)? Choosing purely one or the other leads to failure.

💰
Exploitation
Always pick the action with the highest current Q-value. Called a "greedy move." Maximises short-term reward but risks getting permanently stuck on a suboptimal action.
🔍
Exploration
Deliberately choose a non-greedy action to discover whether something better exists. Sacrifices immediate reward but enables long-term learning and improvement.
🍜 The Noodle Stall Problem

You try the noodle stall on Day 2, but the head chef is absent — the food is mediocre. A purely greedy agent writes off noodles forever. An agent with exploration will occasionally revisit the noodle stall on some random future day and discover the food is actually great when the regular chef is in. Always exploiting means you never escape your first impressions.

Why Pure Exploitation Fails

If you always go with the greedy choice, three things can go wrong. First, your initial sample might have been unlucky — a good action might have had a bad outcome by chance, and you'll never revisit it. Second, the environment might change over time — the action that was best last month might not be best now. Third, there might be an even better action you've never tried because your early experiences led you down a local optimum path.

Why Pure Exploration Also Fails

If you never commit to exploiting what you know, you never build up confidence in any action. You keep collecting data but never actually use it to get good rewards. The agent learns beautifully and performs terribly. Learning without exploitation is information without action — it has no practical value.

🗺️ The Google Maps Analogy

Google Maps shows you the fastest route — that is exploitation. But sometimes you take a different route out of curiosity, or the app reroutes you after a missed turn — that is exploration. The missed turn is a "mistake" that teaches you there is a faster back road. Unless you deviate from the known path occasionally, you never discover that the back road is 10 minutes faster. RL agents need the same occasional tolerance for "mistakes" to escape local optima.

Next: the ε-Greedy strategy — the elegant solution that mixes both.

Lecture 2 · Strategy
The ε-Greedy Strategy
One hyperparameter. Two modes. The simplest solution to the exploration-exploitation dilemma.

The ε-greedy strategy is elegantly simple. Introduce one parameter ε (epsilon) representing the fraction of time steps dedicated to random exploration. With probability 1−ε the agent exploits (picks the current best action). With probability ε the agent explores (picks a completely random action). That is all.

ε-Greedy Rule
With prob. (1−ε): pick argmaxa Q(a)    [exploit]
With prob. ε: pick random action          [explore]

In practice: ε is often started high (explore aggressively early) and decayed over time as the agent gains confidence. A common schedule: start at ε=1.0, decay to ε=0.01 over training. ε is a hyperparameter — you choose it based on the problem.

The Probability Puzzle from Class

This is the exact question raised during the lecture: if you have 2 actions (A1 is greedy, A2 is non-greedy) and ε = 0.5, what is the total probability of choosing A1?

Step-by-step solution

Part 1 (Exploitation): With probability 0.5, you exploit. You definitely choose A1 (greedy). Contributes → 0.50

Part 2 (Exploration): With probability 0.5, you explore. You pick randomly between A1 and A2 — equal chance for each. So A1 gets chosen with probability 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.25

Total P(A1) = 0.50 + 0.25 = 0.75

Even under exploration, the greedy action gets chosen more often than non-greedy, because it benefits from both phases. The general formula: P(greedy) = (1−ε) + ε/K where K is the number of actions.

GreedyAlways pick max Q(a) — pure exploitationGets stuck in local optima; never discovers better options
ε-GreedyExploit with prob 1−ε, random explore with prob εExploration is purely random — inefficient; ε must be tuned

Next: Temporal Difference — how learning actually happens across time steps.

Lecture 2 · Learning Mechanism
Temporal Difference Learning
The same intuition as gradient descent — but learning happens across time, not across a dataset.

At some point in the session, a student asked the natural question: when we say an RL agent "learns," what is actually happening mechanically? Dr. Sanghita answered this by drawing a direct, explicit parallel to the update rule in supervised learning that every student already knows from their previous courses.

The Parallel to Supervised Learning

In supervised learning, the weight update rule is: w_new = w_current + α × (target − prediction). The key idea is that the error — the gap between your prediction and the actual outcome — is used to nudge the model in the right direction, scaled by a learning rate α.

In RL, the same principle applies, but instead of comparing a prediction to a known label, you compare your value estimate at time t to your value estimate at time t+1. The difference between these adjacent-in-time estimates is the TD error. Because learning happens by comparing moments in time, it is called Temporal Difference learning.

AspectSupervised LearningTD Learning (RL)
Core update rulew = w + α × (target − prediction)Q = Q + α × TD_error
What is the error?Prediction vs. known true labelValue estimate at t vs estimate at t+1
When does learning happen?After each batch/epochAfter each time step — continuously
Role of αControls weight update speedSame — a hyperparameter to be tuned

Effect of Different α Values

🚫
α = 0
Estimates never change. The agent starts with its initial guesses and stays there forever. Zero learning — identical to a frozen ML model.
📌
α = constant
Standard approach. Gives slightly more weight to recent rewards — desirable for non-stationary environments where the world changes over time.
📉
α decreasing
Starts high (learn fast while everything is new), reduces over time (trust accumulated experience). Mathematically guaranteed to converge under certain conditions.
Coming Up at Mid-Semester

The full TD update rule with its formal mathematical derivation, including the Bellman equation that underpins it, will be the core topic around your mid-semester exams. Everything you have learned in these first two sessions — policy, value functions, rewards, and the TD principle — will come together in that formulation. Keep these fundamentals sharp.
